Analysis Pipeline

1

Point Pattern Analysis

156 poaching incident points mapped in Tsavo. 100 Monte Carlo simulations run to reject the null hypothesis that the distribution is random — a necessary precondition for building a predictive risk model.

R-STAT + GeoDa (spatial autocorrelation)
2

GIS Risk Map

Physical environment features correlated with poaching incidents: proximity to roads (easiest escape route), proximity to water sources, and land cover type. Risk values assigned per feature based on incident frequency — roads carry the highest risk weighting.

ESRI ArcGIS + Spatial Analyst Extension
3

Drone Flight Path Modelling

UAV surveillance paths planned over high-risk zones. Inputs: cruising altitude, speed, battery/fuel range, and camera horizontal field of view. Outputs: total distance per sortie and the ground coverage area per pass.

ArduPilot Mega Planner
4

Ranger Station Placement

Travel-cost surfaces computed over the Tsavo terrain: roads assigned lowest resistance, dense forest and rivers assigned highest. Optimal guard post locations minimise average response time to the highest-risk poaching zones.

ArcGIS Travel-Cost Surface Analysis

Key Findings

Roads = Highest Risk

Poaching incidents cluster near roads — roads provide both easy access to wildlife and rapid escape routes after poaching. Road proximity was the strongest predictor of incident location in the composite risk map.

Non-Random Distribution

Monte Carlo simulation confirmed the 156 incidents are not randomly distributed — they follow a deterministic spatial pattern tied to terrain features, making the distribution predictable and the risk model valid.

Drone Cost Advantage

Conventional aerial wildlife surveillance costs ~$50,000/week. Drone-based surveillance costs ~$3,000/week — a 17× reduction that makes systematic coverage accessible to small conservation groups with limited budgets.

AI-Assisted Species ID

WildTrack's footprint identification platform (WildTrackAI) achieves >90% species identification accuracy across 14 species, using morphometric analysis on UAV-captured footprint images uploaded to the cloud.
